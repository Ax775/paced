# The 4 phases of your menstrual cycle

Your cycle is more than the days you bleed. It is an ongoing rhythm that roughly
divides into four phases. No cycle is a textbook example — lengths vary from person
to person and month to month — but knowing the broad strokes helps. It lets you spot
patterns and be a little gentler with yourself on the days that ask for it.

Below is a calm explainer of each phase. This is general information, not medical
advice and not a prediction.

## 1. The [menstrual phase](/articles/menstrual-phase)

This is the phase when you menstruate, usually the first days of your cycle. Many
people feel more introverted or tired during these days. Warmth, rest, and
iron-rich foods (like leafy greens or lentils) can feel good. There is nothing wrong
with slowing down.

## 2. The [follicular phase](/articles/follicular-phase)

After your period, your body slowly builds back up. Many people notice a natural
rise in energy and a desire to take things on. It can be a nice moment for new plans
or more active movement — if you feel like it.

## 3. [Ovulation](/articles/ovulation)

Around the middle of your cycle, ovulation takes place. For many people this
coincides with an energy peak and an open, social feeling. It is also the phase
around your fertile window — useful to know, whether you want a pregnancy or not.

## 4. The [luteal phase](/articles/luteal-phase)

After ovulation, in the run-up to your next period, your body turns inward again.
Some people notice more need for rest, or changes in mood, sleep or appetite. Being
gentle with yourself here is not a luxury but logical.

## Why knowing your phase helps

Your cycle phase gives context to what you feel. Tired in the luteal phase? That fits
the rhythm. Full of energy just after your period? That fits too. It is not about
predicting or prescribing, but about learning to listen to your body.
